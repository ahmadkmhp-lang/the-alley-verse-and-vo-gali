# FORTY-SEVEN SECONDS — Part 1 of 3
*Story_01 — THE ALLEY VERSE*

The first one I ever noticed was a video of my sister blowing out her candles.

Thirty-one of them, one for every year, and Lena leaning over the cake with her hair tied back and that crooked grin she only ever gives a camera. I shot it on my phone from two feet away. I remember the smoke. I remember her laughing because the trick candles wouldn't die, relighting themselves while everyone groaned. I remember the exact warmth of that kitchen, the cheap gold "31" balloon knocking against the cupboard, my own thumb in the corner of the frame.

The video doesn't remember any of it.

Forty-one seconds in, the picture stutters — not a glitch, not a freeze, just a clean little cut, as if the world had stopped to take a breath — and when it comes back the candles are already out. The smoke is already a thin grey ribbon climbing to the ceiling. The "31" balloon is on the floor. And Lena isn't looking at the cake anymore. She's looking at me. At the person holding the phone. With an expression I have never once, in thirty-one years, seen on my sister's face: like she is waiting to find out whether I am still there.

Forty-seven seconds. I timed the gap eleven times that first night. Always forty-seven. I count everything now. You will too, by the end of this. That's not a threat. It's just what happens.

I should tell you what I do, because it's the whole reason any of this caught me instead of sliding past the way it slides past everyone else. I restore footage. Old tape, water-damaged film, corrupted CCTV that some lawyer needs cleaned up before a trial. I spend ten hours a day with my face eighteen inches from a monitor, hunting the flaw — the dropped field, the spliced frame, the timestamp that lies. Fifteen years of it. And in fifteen years I built my entire understanding of the world on one quiet article of faith, the kind of thing you don't even know you believe until it breaks:

*The camera doesn't lie. People lie. Memory lies. Footage is the one true thing you can hold in your hand.*

So when the footage and my memory disagreed about my sister's birthday, I did the only natural thing. I sided with the footage. I assumed the broken thing was me.

I want you to notice how reasonable that was. Hold onto it. It's going to stop being reasonable very soon, and I need you to remember that I started out as the sensible one.

I almost let the candle video go. People lose a minute. You zone out, you blink, your brain skips a stone across the surface of an evening. But it nagged at me, so two days later, over dinner, I mentioned it to Lena like a joke. "I think I checked out at your party," I said. "There's like a minute of your video where I just — go blank."

She didn't laugh.

She set her fork down with that specific care she uses when she's choosing her words, the care our mother used to use, and she said, "Dan. You didn't check out. You said something to me."

"When?"

"Right before the candles went out. You leaned in close and you said it, and then you went quiet for the whole rest of the night. You barely talked. You kept looking at the hallway." She tilted her head, studying me the way you study a word you suddenly aren't sure you've spelled right. "You honestly don't remember saying it?"

"Saying what?"

And here is the first small thing that took a piece out of me and kept it. My sister — who fell asleep on my shoulder on the school bus for six years, who gripped my hand white-knuckled at our mother's funeral, who has never in her life been frightened of me — looked down at her plate, and something moved behind her eyes, and she said, very lightly, "It doesn't matter," and asked if I wanted more rice.

It mattered. I could see it mattering all over her face. Whatever I said to her in those forty-seven seconds had landed somewhere deep, and she had decided, right there at the table, not to give it back to me. I have replayed that moment more than I have replayed any piece of footage in my life, and I still cannot tell you whether she was protecting me from what I said — or protecting herself from the fact that I didn't remember saying it.

That night I watched the clip until the sun came up. There is no edit. I want to be precise, because precision is my whole trade: the file is not cut. There is no splice, no re-encode, no tampering signature of any kind. The file is simply forty-seven seconds shorter than the timeline says it should be, and the missing piece is centered, perfectly, on the two of us. As if the absence had measured us first.

I told myself: coincidence, corruption, a phone doing something stupid with its buffer. I am good at telling myself things. It's a related skill to telling other people things.

Then the lock happened, and I got worse at it.

It was the following Tuesday. I closed the lab at 2:47 in the morning — I know the time because I remember being irritated at myself for staying so late again, watching the red 2:47 on the wall clock while I shrugged my coat on. I locked the door behind me. I felt the deadbolt go home under my hand. That detail matters: I *felt* it, the small resistance and then the give, the sound of it. Muscle memory you could swear to in court.

The next morning a courier swore he'd delivered a package that wasn't there, so I pulled our own entry camera — the little fisheye that watches the door from the inside — to check the timestamp.

The footage jumps. Forty-seven seconds, 2:47 to 2:47:47, gone. And on the far side of the gap, the deadbolt is already thrown. The door is already locked. My hand is already coming away from it. And I am already turning to go — except I've stopped, half-turned, and I'm looking back over my shoulder, down the dark length of the hallway behind me, at nothing. There is nothing there. There is no reason on earth to look. But the man in the footage, the man wearing my coat, is looking, with the exact expression my sister wore over the cake.

I sat in my chair in an empty lab and felt the first real hook go in — the question that has not let go of me since, that I suspect will not let go of me even after the thing I can feel coming finally arrives:

*If I don't remember locking that door, but the door is locked — then who locked it? Me? Or the version of me the camera decided to keep, after it deleted the version that I am?*

I did what you would do. I built a roof over my head out of the most comforting material available, and the most comforting material available was the word *fugue.* People black out. Temporal-lobe events, micro-seizures, dissociative episodes — the brain does routine things on autopilot and quietly declines to file the memory. You can lock a door in a fugue. You can say something tender and strange to your sister in a fugue and never know you said it. Best of all, you can take a fugue to a doctor. There is a building you can drive to, with a person in it whose whole job is to make this kind of thing have a name and a cause and, God willing, a prescription.

I made the appointment. I felt better the instant I made it. For about four days I lived inside that warm little room called *it's only me*, where the problem was a fault in one tired man's brain and the rest of the world stayed solid and trustworthy and exactly where I'd left it.

I keep wanting to go back to those four days. They were the last ones where the answer could still be small.

Because here's what I didn't know yet, the thing that was waiting for me at the end of that comfortable week, the thing that turned the lock in the door of my whole life:

A brain can lose forty-seven seconds. I'd accept that. Brains are soft, fallible things; mine had every right to skip.

But a brain cannot reach up into a ceiling-mounted camera on a separate circuit, recording to a server in another room, and pinch the same forty-seven seconds out of *that.* A brain cannot lean into a cloud backup on a phone that was face-down in my pocket and quietly remove the identical window, down to the second, on a night I wasn't even the thing being filmed.

And when I finally went looking — when I stopped trusting that the gap was mine and started pulling every recording I could find from those exact timestamps — I discovered that the forty-seven seconds was not a hole in my memory at all.

It was a hole in the record.

And it was getting bigger.
