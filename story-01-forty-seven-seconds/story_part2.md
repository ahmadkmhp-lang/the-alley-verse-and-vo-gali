# FORTY-SEVEN SECONDS — Part 2 of 3
*Story_01 — THE ALLEY VERSE*

It started with three machines that had no reason to agree.

The night the lock happened, four cameras were running near me, and not one of them belonged to me. There was the lab's ceiling unit, bolted to a joist and wired to a server two rooms away. There was the cloud backup on my phone, which had been face-down in my coat pocket, filming the inside of dark wool. There was the cheap dashcam in my car, which wakes on motion. And there was the building's lobby system, which I have no login for and have never touched.

I pulled all four for the window from 2:46 to 2:48.

All four jump. The same forty-seven seconds. 2:47:00 to 2:47:47, gone, on four devices that share no circuit, no clock, no network — devices that have never once in their lives been synchronized to anything. A phone's clock drifts from a building's by whole seconds. A dashcam doesn't know what year it is. And yet the hole in each of them began and ended on the same instant, as if all four had been told the same secret at the same time and all four had agreed, politely, to look away together.

I am an archivist. I want you to understand what that fact did to me, professionally, in my body. There is no edit that does this. There is no corruption that does this. Corruption is messy and local and stupid; it eats a file from one direction. This was *surgical,* and it was *coordinated,* and it did not live inside my brain, because my brain has never had administrative access to a building I don't work in.

That was the night the warm little room called *it's only me* went dark, and I stepped out of it into the cold, and the cold is where I have lived ever since.

* * *

The cup is the part nobody believes, so let me be careful with it, the way I'd be careful with evidence I had to defend in court.

I knocked a mug of cold coffee off my desk with my elbow. The dumbest accident. I watched it fall, watched it burst on the lab floor, swore, and got up to find the broom. While I was up, my phone — sitting face-up on the desk — did the small automatic thing phones do, woke for a moment, and saved a still to the cloud. Timestamp: 2:47 PM.

I knocked the cup over at 2:51. I am certain of 2:51, because the lab clock is in the frame of the CCTV I checked afterward, and the CCTV shows the mug sitting whole and full on my desk at 2:47, four minutes before my elbow ever found it.

But the photo my phone saved at 2:47 — sealed, hashed, its metadata a clean unbroken line — shows the cup already on the floor. Already in pieces. And not *a* broken cup; *the* broken cup. The same starburst of shards. The same dark splash of coffee reaching toward the door. The one big curved handle-fragment that, four minutes in the future, would come to rest nearest the threshold. I crouched on that floor with my phone in one hand and the real shards under the other and matched them piece for piece, and they matched, because the photograph was not a prediction. It was a memory. The record remembered the cup breaking before the cup had broken.

And there, in the corner of the frame — behind the shards, in the dark slot beneath the supply shelf where there has only ever been a painted cinderblock wall — there was a door.

Narrow. Far too narrow for a person to pass through. Open a hand's width onto a black that wasn't shadow and wasn't a room, the particular black of a monitor that has just been switched off. I went and put my own hands on that wall. Solid. Cold. No door. There has never been a door.

But it was in the photograph. And once my eye had learned its shape, I couldn't stop finding it — a sliver in the reflection on a dead screen, a thin seam at the very edge of my sister's candle video, always small, always in the margin, always near a clock or a counter reading 47. Never the thing the lens meant to capture. Always the thing it caught by accident, the way you catch a word in a language you didn't know you were starting to understand.

I'd love to tell you I had never seen that door before.

* * *

His name was Marcus, and I have been not telling you about him, and I think you've started to feel the shape of him the way you feel a missing tooth with your tongue.

Nine years ago, before the lab, I freelanced. Marcus hired me to restore some home videos — degraded, nothing strange, or so we both thought. But there was a gap in them. Forty-seven seconds, recurring, threaded back through years of his life. I found it. I was twenty-five and proud and it was the most fascinating puzzle I had ever been handed, so I did the thing I am good at, the only thing I have ever truly been good at: I dug. I reconstructed. I dragged those forty-seven-second holes up into the light and *restored* them, frame by frame, because that is what restoration is — refusing to let an absence stay an absence.

I never told Marcus to stop. I never warned him, because I didn't know there was anything to warn him about.

And then there was no Marcus.

Not dead. I need you to hear the difference, because the difference is the whole horror. One week I had a client named Marcus, a folder of his footage, an invoice with his name on it. The next week the folder wore a different name, the invoice was blank in the place his name had been, and the people who'd referred him looked at me with patient confusion when I said the word "Marcus," the way Lena looked at me across the rice — *waiting to find out if I was still there.* No record of him survives. None. Except a single frame I'd exported and kept, of a man at a kitchen table — and I checked that frame last month, and now even it has a forty-seven-second-shaped absence where his face used to be. A smear. A seam. A door.

So when you ask what I'm hiding, here it is, laid on the table: I have watched this happen to someone before. I know how it ends. And the last time, I was not the one it happened to.

The last time, I was the one holding the camera. I was the one who wouldn't let the gap stay empty.

* * *

I made one more mistake. I told two coworkers.

Priya works the bench beside mine; Sam runs the audio booth. Both were in the room the afternoon a gap happened during working hours — 2:47, always 2:47 — and afterward I asked them, apart, what they'd seen.

Priya said I never moved. She said I sat right there the entire time, talking to her about a render queue, perfectly ordinary, and that the only strange thing was how "paranoid" I got about it later. She quoted sentences I supposedly said. She was completely certain.

Sam said I stood up and walked out. He said the lights died for about a minute — he had to save his session by feel, in the dark — and that when they came back I was standing in the doorway "looking at everyone like I was counting heads." He was a little unnerved telling me, and Sam does not unnerve.

Same room. Same forty-seven seconds. One of them watched me sit and speak. One of them watched me vanish into the dark. Both certain. Both, I would swear, honest. So whose memory is the false one — hers, his, mine? Or — and this is the thought that began waking me at exactly 2:47 every night, no alarm — is it possible that none of them is false? That for forty-seven seconds there was more than one version of that room, and the only reason we usually agree about what happened is that, usually, nothing reaches in and edits the world while we're not looking?

After that day, Priya stopped taking lunch with me. Sam started using the far stairs. No one was unkind. No one said a word. They simply cooled, the way a house cools when a door's been left open somewhere you can't find, and I learned the loneliest fact of my life: people who share a gap with me drift away from me afterward, gently, permanently, and not one of them can tell you why.

* * *

The keys are when I understood the thing had teeth.

I keep them on a hook by the lab door. One afternoon, trying to time exactly how long a gap took, I pulled two feeds for the same minute — the door camera and the lobby turnstile three floors down. And in the same verified second, my keys are hanging on the hook by the door, and my keys are sitting in the turnstile tray three floors below. The same keys. The brass tag I scratched my own initials into at nineteen. In two places. At one instant. Before I had so much as touched them that day.

I have shown that footage to the only person alive who can read footage the way I can, which is me, and I cannot break it. Both files are real. Both hashes match the originals. There is no edit. My keys were in two places at once, and forty seconds later I lifted them off the hook the way I do every evening and walked out into a world that had quietly stopped being able to hold a single fact in a single place — and the turnstile version simply stopped being something anyone but me remembers.

That night I sat under the wall of photographs and timestamps I'd started building in my flat, red string running corner to corner, and I admitted the question I'd been refusing for weeks. Not *what is happening to me.* A worse one.

*What if it isn't happening to me at all? What if I am the place it happens?*

And then my phone lit up on the desk, and it was Lena, and she wanted to come over, and I should have said no.
